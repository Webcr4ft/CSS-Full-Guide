# What Are Best Practices for Designing Breadcrumbs?

> **NOTE:** Some of the interactive examples may contain CSS that you haven't learned yet. Don't worry about understanding every line of code. The purpose of the examples is to demonstrate good breadcrumb design principles rather than teach CSS syntax.

---

# What Are Breadcrumbs?

**Breadcrumbs** are a secondary navigation aid that shows users where they are within a website's hierarchy.

They display the path from the homepage to the current page, making it easy for users to understand their location and navigate back to previous sections.

For example:

`Home > Electronics > Phones > Smartphone XYZ`

In this example:

* **Home** is the starting page.
* **Electronics** is the main category.
* **Phones** is a subcategory.
* **Smartphone XYZ** is the current page.

---

# Why Are They Called Breadcrumbs?

The name comes from the fairy tale **Hansel and Gretel**, where the children dropped breadcrumbs along the path so they could find their way back home.

Similarly, website breadcrumbs help users retrace their navigation path.

---

# Why Are Breadcrumbs Important?

Breadcrumbs improve navigation by helping users:

* Understand where they are within the website.
* See the relationship between pages.
* Quickly return to previous sections.
* Explore related categories.
* Reduce confusion on large websites.

They provide context, especially on websites with many pages and categories.

---

# How Breadcrumbs Work

Breadcrumbs display a hierarchical path instead of showing browsing history.

For example:

`Home > Electronics > Phones > Smartphone XYZ`

This indicates that:

1. The product belongs to the **Phones** category.
2. Phones belong to **Electronics**.
3. Electronics belong to the **Home** section.

Each previous level is usually a clickable link, while the current page is often displayed as plain text.

---

# Where Should Breadcrumbs Be Placed?

Breadcrumbs are typically positioned near the top of the page.

Most websites place them:

* Below the main navigation bar.
* Above the page title.
* Near the beginning of the page content.

Users should be able to locate breadcrumbs immediately without searching for them.

---

# Why Placement Matters

Good placement helps users:

* Instantly understand where they are.
* Navigate more efficiently.
* Return to previous categories with fewer clicks.

If breadcrumbs are hidden or placed too low on the page, many users may never notice them.

---

# Best Practice #1: Choose a Clear Separator

A **separator** is the symbol placed between each breadcrumb.

Common separators include:

* `>`
* `/`
* `»`

For example:

`Home > Electronics > Phones > Smartphone XYZ`

or

`Home / Electronics / Phones / Smartphone XYZ`

Choose one style and use it consistently throughout the website.

---

# Why Separators Matter

Separators help users clearly distinguish each navigation level.

A good separator should:

* Be easy to recognize.
* Be visually simple.
* Avoid distracting users.
* Clearly communicate hierarchy.

---

# Best Practice #2: Make Breadcrumbs Easy to Find

Breadcrumbs should always be visible.

Avoid hiding them among other interface elements.

Users should never have to search for breadcrumb navigation.

Good visibility improves usability and saves time.

---

# Best Practice #3: Use an Appropriate Size

Breadcrumbs should be:

* Easy to read.
* Smaller than the main heading.
* Smaller than primary navigation.

Remember, breadcrumbs are a **secondary navigation tool**, not the primary navigation menu.

If they are too large, they compete with more important content.

If they are too small, users may ignore them.

---

# Breadcrumbs Are Secondary Navigation

Primary navigation includes:

* Navigation bars
* Sidebars
* Menus

Breadcrumbs simply provide additional navigation support.

Their purpose is to help users understand their location—not replace the main navigation.

---

# When Should You Use Breadcrumbs?

Breadcrumbs work best on websites with a **deep hierarchy**.

A deep hierarchy means users must navigate through several levels before reaching specific content.

Examples include:

* Online stores (E-commerce)
* News websites
* Documentation websites
* Learning platforms
* Knowledge bases

These websites contain many categories and subcategories, making breadcrumbs very useful.

---

# When Breadcrumbs Are Not Necessary

Breadcrumbs are usually unnecessary on websites with a **shallow hierarchy**.

A shallow hierarchy means users only navigate through one or two levels.

Examples include:

* Personal portfolios
* Small business websites
* Landing pages
* Simple blogs
* Single-page websites

Adding breadcrumbs in these situations often provides little benefit.

---

# Advantages of Breadcrumbs

Well-designed breadcrumbs:

* Improve navigation.
* Help users understand page relationships.
* Reduce confusion.
* Save users time.
* Encourage exploration.
* Improve the overall user experience.

They can also contribute to better usability and search engine optimization (SEO) when implemented correctly.

---

# Best Practices Summary

| Best Practice | Why It Matters |
|---------------|----------------|
| Place breadcrumbs near the top of the page | Makes them easy to find. |
| Use clear separators | Improves readability and hierarchy. |
| Keep them smaller than primary navigation | Maintains visual hierarchy. |
| Make previous pages clickable | Allows quick navigation. |
| Show the current page last | Clearly indicates the user's location. |
| Use breadcrumbs on large websites | Helps users navigate deep site structures. |
| Avoid breadcrumbs on simple websites | Prevents unnecessary interface clutter. |

---

# Conclusion

Breadcrumbs are a simple but powerful navigation feature.

They help users understand where they are, how pages are related, and how to navigate back through a website's hierarchy.

When designed with clear separators, proper placement, appropriate sizing, and consistent styling, breadcrumbs significantly improve usability on websites with large amounts of content and deep navigation structures.
